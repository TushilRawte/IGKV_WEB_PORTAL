NSS Overview

In May 1969, a conference of student representatives organized by the Ministry of Education and the University Grants Commission also unanimously agreed that the National Service Scheme could be a means for national integration. Details were soon prepared, and the Planning Commission approved the expenditure. During the fourth and fifth plan periods, Rs. 5 crores were earmarked for NSS, with the plan commission determining that NSS would be a pilot project in selected institutions and universities. On September 14, 1969, the then Union Education Minister V.K.R.V. Rao inaugurated NSS in 37 universities across all states. This scheme has been implemented in all states of the country, as well as in many states in premier institutions.


History

 The National Service Scheme (NSS) was launched in 1969 during Mahatma Gandhi's birth centenary year, involving 40,000 students from 37 universities. NSS is an extension dimension of the higher education system that encourages students to be oriented towards community service while studying in educational institutions. It is being implemented by the Ministry of Youth Affairs and Sports, Government of India. Following independence, the University Grants Commission, under the chairmanship of S. Radhakrishnan, recommended initiating voluntary national service in educational institutions. The Central Advisory Board of Education (CABE) reconsidered this idea in January 1950. After examining the thoughts and experiences of other countries in this area, the board suggested that students and teachers should devote voluntary manual time. In a draft plan adopted by the government in the first five-year plan in 1952, Indian students were encouraged to contribute to social and labor services for one year. In 1958, Jawaharlal Nehru, in a letter to the Chief Ministers, considered social service as a prerequisite for undergraduate education. He directed the Ministry of Education to prepare a suitable plan to initiate national service in educational institutions.


Objective Of NSS

  • The objective of the program is to involve students in social welfare and provide unbiased service to society.

  • NSS volunteers work to ensure that everyone in need receives assistance to improve their quality of life and live with dignity.

  • In doing so, volunteers learn from the people in villages how to lead a good life despite limited resources.

  • The program emphasizes the importance of selfless service and fostering a sense of community among participants.

  • Through NSS activities, students gain practical experience in social work and develop empathy towards the less fortunate.

  • NSS aims to instill in students a sense of responsibility towards society and encourage them to actively contribute to its betterment.

Aims Of NSS

  • The ideal statement or slogan of the National Service Scheme is "Not I, but you."

  • It epitomizes the essence of democratic life and emphasizes the need for selfless service and appreciation of others' perspectives.

  • It also reflects upon the notion that an individual's welfare ultimately depends on the overall welfare of society, as showcased by promoting this ideal statement in everyday activities.

  • The NSS aims to instill these values among its participants and promote a sense of responsibility towards the welfare of fellow human beings.

  • Through this slogan, the NSS encourages individuals to prioritize the well-being of others over their own, fostering a culture of empathy and community service.

The symbol of the National Service Scheme

The symbol of the National Service Scheme is based on the "Rath" wheel of the Konark Sun Temple in Odisha. The navy-blue color signifies the universe, of which the NSS is a small part, ready to contribute its share for the welfare of humanity. The red color in the emblem indicates that NSS volunteers are filled with blood, meaning they are vibrant, active, energetic, and filled with high spirits. The massive wheels of the Sun Temple depict the cycle of creation, preservation, and release, symbolizing movement in life across time and space.
